    In the late 1960s/early 1970s, a group of left-wing young and controversial poets, coming on the heels of movements such as the Black Mountain, the New American poets, the San Francisco renaissance and the Beats, would come to form an avant-garde post-modernist school of poetry, gaining recognition as the Language poets. Language poetry aimed to place complete emphasis on the language of the poem and to create a new way for the reader to interact with the work. It downplayed expression, seeing the poem as a construction in and of language itself.

    Two magazines are commonly associated with the birth and evolution of these innovators: This and L=A=N=G=U=A=G=E. The latter, published by Bruce Andrews and Charles Bernstein between February 1978 and October 1981, is available to read in it's entirety on Eclipse Archive.

    If you're interested, there are a handful of books on Postmodernist Literature and papers on the Language poets themselves that outline and analyize the characteristics and tendencies of this lesser-known group of American poets. This is also an interesting read.
